What the story shows

  • David chooses safety over truth.
  • Giovanni is abandoned and falls into despair.
  • Hella is hurt by David’s dishonesty and leaves.
  • David is left alone with a guilt that cannot be torn up and thrown away.

Theme: Identity & Self-Expression. David’s fear of expressing his true identity damages his relationships and leaves him with guilt. Baldwin’s novel asks what it costs when we hide who we are in order to be accepted.

For the Teacher

This is a short walk-through game based on James Baldwin’s Giovanni’s Room, told in five scenes. Here’s how it covers the assignment:

  • Characters: David, Giovanni, Hella, David’s father, Jacques, and Guillaume all show up and have lines.
  • Plot: the night in the south of France; David’s childhood, the Joey memory, and his father; meeting Giovanni at the bar; living in Giovanni’s room; Hella coming back and David staying quiet; Guillaume’s death and Giovanni’s sentence; Hella leaving; David alone at the end.
  • Central conflict: being honest about who you are vs. doing what’s safe and accepted.
  • Theme: identity and self-expression.
  • Quotes: six evidence cards you collect, each a direct quote from the book with its Part and Chapter and a page citation, e.g. (Baldwin 17). I listed Part and Chapter too, since page numbers change between editions.
  • Interactivity: you walk around in first person, look at objects, pick up the quote cards, answer a question at the end of each scene, and make dialogue choices.

Creator’s Reflection

I went with a walk-through game instead of a normal quiz because it seemed more interesting, and I figured people would pay more attention if they had to explore and make choices instead of just clicking answers.

Writing the questions and picking the quote cards was probably the longest part. Sometimes it was hard to find questions that weren’t too easy but also weren’t impossible. I ended up using different parts of the book and some quotes, because otherwise the scenes started to feel repetitive.

The ending was one of the last things I made. At first the final choice was simpler, but I changed it because I wanted the ending to feel a little more satisfying.

This made me go back through the novel more than I expected, since I had to check details and make sure the answers actually worked. That probably took the most time.

I think the most important thing here is understanding the characters and why they make their decisions. If you only know a basic summary of the book, some parts are harder than they look.

Mostly I just wanted to make something a little more fun than a regular assignment.
